The Signal in the Static

The radio crackled to life at 3:47 AM, filling Dr. Elena Chen's cramped laboratory with the sound of something that wasn't quite noise. She'd been monitoring the frequency for seven months, ever since the first anomaly appeared in the data.

"There," she whispered, adjusting the gain. The pattern was unmistakable now—not random static, but structured interference. Something was broadcasting from beyond the solar system.

The Discovery

She'd almost missed it. The signal was buried deep in the cosmic background radiation, hidden like a whisper in a hurricane. But Elena had always been good at finding whispers.

"You're not supposed to be there," she said to the oscilloscope, watching the waveform pulse with an almost biological rhythm. "Nothing man-made, nothing natural... so what are you?"

The pattern repeated every 47.3 seconds. Too regular to be coincidence. Too complex to be noise.

Three weeks later, the decoding algorithm finally cracked the first layer. What it revealed changed everything:

The message wasn't from space. > It was being reflected back to us.

Someone—or something—had heard humanity's first radio broadcasts from a century ago. And they were sending them back, modified, annotated, answered.

Elena stared at the decoded text on her screen, her coffee growing cold in her trembling hands. After all these years of searching, of hoping, of wondering if we were alone...

The universe had finally said hello.

And it had questions of its own.
